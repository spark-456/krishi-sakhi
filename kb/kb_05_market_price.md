### 1.2 How to Access Price Information
- **Telangana Rashtra Rythu Seva Kendras (TRRSK)**: Mandal-level offices with price display boards.
- **mKisan Portal**: SMS-based price alerts from Government of India (sms 'MANDI [crop name]' to 51969).
- **Agmarknet**: https://agmarknet.gov.in — daily mandi prices across India.
- **Telangana Agriculture Department**: https://agriculture.telangana.gov.in
- **e-NAM (National Agriculture Market)**: https://enam.gov.in — online mandi trading; 76 mandis in Telangana onboarded.
- **Kisan Suvidha App**: Government of India app with local mandi prices, weather, crop advisory.

## SECTION 3: Historical Price Trends and Seasonal Patterns

### 3.1 Paddy (Rice) — Seasonal Price Pattern

| Month | Typical Paddy Price Trend | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| October–November | Sharp decline (₹1800–2000/q) | Kharif arrival; large volumes |
| December–January | Stabilize (₹2000–2200/q) | Market absorption; procurement begins |
| February–March | Moderate rise (₹2100–2400/q) | Post-procurement; supply reduction |
| April–June | Higher (₹2200–2600/q) | Lean season; pre-Kharif arrival |
| July–September | Stable to rising | Inter-season; last stocks being cleared |

**Farmer Advisory:** Avoid distress selling immediately at harvest (Oct–Nov). If storage is available, holding paddy for 4–6 weeks post-harvest typically yields ₹150–300/quintal higher prices. PACS (Primary Agriculture Cooperative Societies) offer warehousing in many mandals.

### 3.2 Cotton — Seasonal Price Pattern

| Month | Typical Price Range (₹/quintal, seed cotton) | Remarks |
|---|---|---|
| October–November | 6000–6500 | Early Kharif arrival; ginners hold back |
| December–January | 6500–7500 | Peak arrivals; CCI procurement active |
| February–March | 7000–8000 | Supplies declining; mills compete |
| April–June | 8000–9500 | Very low supply; carryover stock premium |
| July–September | 7500–8500 | Pre-season; depends on export demand |

**Cotton Market Factors:**
- International cotton price (NY ICE Futures, COTLOOK A Index) heavily influences India mandi prices.
- Rupee depreciation: Generally positive for cotton prices (export competitiveness).
- Polyester vs cotton price spread: Wide spread → textile mills shift to cotton → price up.

### 3.3 Groundnut — Seasonal Price Pattern

| Month | Price Trend (₹/quintal, pods) | Remarks |
|---|---|---|
| October–November | 5000–5500 | Kharif harvest arrival |
| December–February | 5500–6500 | Gradual rise; NAFED procurement |
| March–May | 6500–7500 | Stock depletion; edible oil demand |
| June–September | 6000–7000 | Variable; depends on imports and monsoon |

**Edible Oil Price Link:** Groundnut price is strongly linked to edible oil import policy. When government lowers import duty on palm oil/soyoil, groundnut oil demand falls → groundnut mandi prices drop.

### 3.4 Chilli — Price Volatility

Chilli prices are extremely volatile — highest CV (coefficient of variation) among all Telangana crops.

| Month | Typical Wholesale Price Range (₹/quintal, dry) | Remarks |
|---|---|---|
| January–February | 6000–10000 | Rabi harvest; new season supply |
| March–April | 8000–14000 | Post-harvest; quality-driven variation |
| May–July | 10000–18000 | Stock depletion; export demand |
| August–September | 8000–12000 | Lean; depends on arrivals |
| October–November | 6000–9000 | Second Rabi harvest (some areas) |

**Guntur (AP) Price:**
Guntur chilli market (Mirchi Yard) is the single largest chilli market in Asia. Telangana prices closely track Guntur prices with a 10–20% basis discount for quality difference.

- Source: Agmarknet historical data; NAFED Market Intelligence; NCDEX (commodity exchange) price history; DPIIT export data

## SECTION 4: Grade and Quality Standards for Mandi Sale

### 4.1 Paddy — Quality Standards for MSP Procurement

| Parameter | Specification | Rejection Level |
|---|---|---|
| Moisture content | Max 17% (FAO standard) | > 25% rejected |
| Foreign matter | Max 1% | > 2% attracts deduction |
| Damaged grains | Max 5% | > 8% downgraded |
| Immature grains | Max 3% | > 5% deduction |
| Husk/weed seeds | Max 1% | > 2% deduction |

**Moisture Testing:**
- Portable moisture meters available at every procurement center.
- Ideal harvest moisture: 18–22%; dry to 14% for storage; 17% for immediate sale.

### 4.2 Cotton — Quality Standards

| Grade | Staple Length (mm) | Micronaire | Ginning Out-turn (%) | Premium/Discount |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Long Staple (LS) | > 32 | 3.5–4.9 | > 37 | +₹400/q premium |
| Medium Staple (MS) | 26–32 | 3.5–4.9 | 33–37 | Base MSP |
| Short Staple (SS) | < 26 | > 5.5 | < 33 | -₹200–500/q discount |

**Common Quality Issues:**
- High moisture (> 10%): Ginning rejection; mold risk in storage.
- Trash content > 8%: Discount at ginnery.
- Contamination (polypropylene fibers from bags): ZERO tolerance in export quality; heavy penalty at ginnery.

**Critical:** Never mix plastic/polythene bags or ropes with cotton during picking, storage, or transport. Polypropylene contamination is a serious quality issue damaging India's export reputation.

### 4.3 Groundnut — Quality Standards

| Parameter | Export Grade | Domestic Grade |
|---|---|---|
| Moisture (in-shell) | < 7% | < 9% |
| Aflatoxin B1 | < 2 ppb (EU) / < 15 ppb (India) | < 30 ppb |
| Foreign matter | < 1% | < 2% |
| Shriveled pods | < 3% | < 8% |

**Aflatoxin Risk:**
- Aflatoxin contamination is the major constraint for groundnut export.
- Risk factors: Late harvest, delayed drying, improper storage, pre-harvest drought stress.
- Prevention: Harvest at 70–80% mature pods; dry to 7% moisture within 3 days; store in well-ventilated gunny bags.
- Source: APEDA Groundnut Export Quality Standards; FSSAI Aflatoxin Limits; EU Regulation (EC) 1881/2006

### 4.4 Chilli — Quality Standards

| Parameter | Premium (Red Chilli) | Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Moisture | < 10% | < 12% |
| Color (ASTA units) | > 120 | > 80 |
| Capsaicin content | > 0.5% | Variable |
| Aflatoxin | < 5 ppb (EU) | < 20 ppb (FSSAI) |
| Foreign matter | < 2% | < 5% |

ASTA (American Spice Trade Association) color units determine price in export market. Guntur S4 variety commands premium for high ASTA color.

## SECTION 5: Storage and Warehousing

### 5.1 On-Farm Storage Guidelines

| Crop | Ideal Moisture for Storage | Storage Duration (Safe) | Pests to Watch | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Paddy (milled rice) | < 14% | 6–12 months | Rice weevil, grain moth | Malathion 0.1% spray on walls |
| Wheat | < 12% | 12–18 months | Khapra beetle, weevil | Aluminium phosphide fumigation (2 tablets/t) |
| Groundnut (pods) | < 7% | 6–12 months | Bruchid, Aspergillus | Store in jute bags; aerate |
| Chickpea | < 9% | 12–24 months | Bruchid | Edible oil coating (5 mL/kg) prevents bruchid |
| Maize | < 12% | 6–12 months | Weevil, AFB | Hermetic storage (Purdue Improved Cowpea Storage) |
| Cotton (seed cotton) | < 10% | 2–4 months | Nil | Dry thoroughly; keep bales dry |
| Chilli (dry) | < 10% | 12–18 months | Mites, storage moths | Sulfuryl fluoride fumigation or controlled atmosphere |

### 5.2 Warehouse Receipt System (WRS) — Telangana

**Warehouse Receipt System** allows farmers to store produce in licensed warehouses and access credit against stored goods without selling immediately. Useful for price realization optimization.

- Implementing agencies: WDRA (Warehouse Development and Regulatory Authority) — wdra.nic.in
- PACS (Primary Agriculture Cooperative Societies) warehouses in mandals
- Telangana State Cooperative Marketing Federation (TSCMF)
- Bank loan against WRS: Up to 75% of market value as crop loan; 7% interest under KCC

- Source: WDRA; TSCMF; RBI Agricultural Credit Guidelines

## SECTION 6: Government Schemes for Market Support

### 6.1 Rythu Bandhu (Telangana)
- Investment support: ₹5000/acre per season (Kharif + Rabi = ₹10,000/acre/year).
- Benefit: Direct cash transfer before sowing season to buy inputs.
- Eligibility: Pattadar (landowner) farmers; 6 million beneficiaries.
- Mode: DBT to farmer's bank account linked to Aadhar and land records.

### 6.2 PM-Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY)
- Crop insurance: Premium 1.5–2% for Rabi; 2% for Kharif; Actuarial premium balance paid by GOI + State.
- Coverage: Sowing failure, mid-season adversity, post-harvest losses (14 days).
- Claims: Via Krishi Vigyan Kendra assessment or remote sensing trigger.
- Enrollment: Through bank branch (if availing KCC loan — mandatory); others voluntary.
- Telangana status: Participating state.

### 6.3 e-NAM — National Agriculture Market
- Electronic platform for mandi trade; price discovery through online bidding.
- Telangana mandis onboarded: 76 APMCs.
- Farmer can sell from registered mandi without physical presence.
- Payment within 24 hours directly to bank account.
- Registration: Through Arhtia (commission agent) at mandi or online via enam.gov.in.

### 6.4 KCC (Kisan Credit Card)
- Revolving credit for crop inputs; livestock; post-harvest.
- Limit: Based on land holding and scale of finance (district-level); typically ₹50,000–3 lakh.
- Interest: 7% per annum (with 3% subvention if repaid on time = effective 4%).
- Eligible crops: All notified crops; interest subvention limited to short-term crop loan < ₹3 lakh.

- Source: Telangana Agriculture Dept Scheme Documents; PMFBY Guidelines; NAFED; e-NAM portal

## SECTION 7: Export Channels for Telangana Produce

| Commodity | Key Export Markets | Export Standard | Export Agency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chilli (dry) | Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, USA, EU, Malaysia | APEDA Residue Monitoring; FSSAI | APEDA; Guntur exporters |
| Groundnut | EU, Canada, Japan, Middle East | < 2 ppb Aflatoxin B1; < 7% moisture | APEDA; private exporters |
| Turmeric | USA, EU, UAE | Curcumin > 3.5%; aflatoxin < 10 ppb | Erode/Nizamabad trade |
| Rice (Basmati) | Middle East, EU, USA | APEDA quality; no specific MRL violations | APEDA registered millers |
| Maize | Bangladesh, SE Asia | Aflatoxin < 10 ppb; moisture < 14% | Private traders |
| Sesame | Japan, China, EU, Middle East | Sesame-specific MRLs (Japan very strict) | APEDA |

**Export Quality Pre-requisite for Farmers:**
1. Follow Good Agricultural Practices (GAP) — pesticide PHI compliance mandatory.
2. Maintain farm records for traceability.
3. Sell to APEDA-registered exporters / processors for premium.
4. For EU/Japan: Third-party residue testing certificate may be required.

- Source: APEDA Export Statistics; DGFT (Directorate General of Foreign Trade); FSSAI; EU RASFF (Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed) India rejection data
